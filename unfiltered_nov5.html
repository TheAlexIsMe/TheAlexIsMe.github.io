The Exhausting Performance of Online Authenticity

Dispatch Log: November 5, 2025 | Data Stream: School & Theory

We’ve all seen the trend: a grainy, "unfiltered" photo, a video confession tearfully detailing personal struggles, or a candid caption swearing off the "curated aesthetic." The call for authenticity online has become louder than ever. But what happens when authenticity itself becomes the next commodity?

The Algorithm Loves "Real" (But Not Too Real)

My Digital Media theory class recently discussed how platforms like TikTok and Instagram incentivize content that *looks* spontaneous and genuine. The old, highly polished, airbrushed look is out. The new algorithm favors the "raw moment." But this shift isn't about liberation; it's about control and monetization.

When platforms push for "authenticity," they are defining the parameters of what *acceptable vulnerability* looks like, turning our genuine struggles into engagement metrics.

We are now performing our exhaustion, scheduling our "spontaneous" breakdowns, and carefully crafting the image of someone who doesn't try too hard. This is arguably more draining than maintaining the old perfect façade.

The Price of "Unfiltered"

Every time a creator shares a "day in the life" where they show their messy room or forget their coffee, it serves two purposes: it lowers the bar for audience expectation (making the audience feel better) and boosts their engagement (making the creator more money). The performance of *not* performing is now the highest form of performance.

As I navigate my media coursework and try to build a professional-but-still-human presence, I realize the only way to genuinely be "unfiltered" is to opt out of the system entirely. Hence, this blog—no face, no performance, just the perspective.

— Alex
