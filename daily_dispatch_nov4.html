Stolen Scooters, Revoked FAFSA, and the View from The Cage

Posted on November 4, 2025 | By Alex | Daily Dispatch

Yesterday felt like a perfect split screen: one side was overwhelming stress, and the other was genuinely good, distracting fun. It’s the kind of contrast that makes you question how the world manages to deliver such polar opposite experiences in the span of 24 hours.

The Headache: The Bureaucratic Nightmare

Let's start with the stress. For the last week, I’ve been running into endless walls trying to deal with financial aid. It turns out my FAFSA has been temporarily **revoked**. The reasoning is buried deep in bureaucratic jargon, something about a missing signature or an old piece of documentation that was supposedly submitted months ago.

The fear isn’t just the money; it’s the thought that one clerical error could completely derail my semester and waste all the effort I’ve put in so far. It makes college feel less like an investment and more like a high-stakes, easily sabotaged gamble. I spent three hours on hold and got nowhere.

And speaking of things that get stolen, my scooter finally bit the dust. Or rather, it was **stolen**. I swear, trying to be a responsible student with transportation in this city is a constant battle against sheer chaos. That scooter was my lifeline, and now I'm back to relying on inconsistent public transit, compounding the feeling that everything is moving backward.

The Escape: The Visual Culture of The Cage

After that, I desperately needed a break. I went to the visual arts building, specifically the photo lab, which we call "The Cage" because of the chain-link walls. That room is always humming with low-level stress and high-level creativity. I was watching a friend work on a project involving **long-exposure photography** using only colored gels and light painting. It was mesmerizing.

We got talking about the difference between *visual noise* (like what you see plastered all over social media feeds) and *intentional visual culture*. The work in The Cage felt intentional. It was slow, frustrating, and required precision—the exact opposite of the chaotic energy of the outside world that had just claimed my FAFSA and my scooter. It was a refreshing reminder that the theory we study in class actually translates into something meaningful, even if my professor doesn't always seem to grade it that way (a subject for another post, perhaps).

Sometimes, all it takes is seeing a **single, focused effort**—like a long-exposure shot coming together—to regain perspective when the rest of your life feels like a blur.
